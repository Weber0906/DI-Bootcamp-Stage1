Frank Sinatra was an American singer, actor, and producer, who was born on December 12, 1915, in Hoboken, New Jersey, and passed away on May 14, 1998, in Los Angeles, California.
Sinatra began his music career in the swing era with the Harry James and Tommy Dorsey orchestras in the 1930s.
He rose to fame as a solo artist in the 1940s, with hits like "All or Nothing at All" and "I'll Never Smile Again." He became known for his smooth, sophisticated vocal style and became known as "The Voice."
In the 1950s, Sinatra continued to top the charts with hits like "Young at Heart," "Witchcraft," and "I've Got You Under My Skin." He also began to focus on acting, and starred in films such as "From Here to Eternity," which won him an Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor, "The Man with the Golden Arm," and "Guys and Dolls."
Sinatra's career continued to flourish into the 1960s and beyond, as he recorded more popular songs like "My Way," "Strangers in the Night," and "New York, New York." He also continued his acting career, appearing in films like "The Manchurian Candidate" and "Ocean's 11." Sinatra was known for his personal life as well, which was often the subject of tabloid headlines. He was married four times, and had numerous affairs throughout his life. He was also involved in politics, and was a supporter of the Democratic Party. Throughout his career, Sinatra sold over 150 million records worldwide and won multiple Grammy Awards. He was also honored with the Presidential Medal of Freedom and the Congressional Gold Medal. He remains an iconic figure in American music and culture.
